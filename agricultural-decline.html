Agricultural Decline​

Failures in Collectivizing Agriculture

Under Mao, the communist party seized private farms, claimed them as public property, and turned them into communal farms.

Poor Management and Organization

Most produce was taken as part of government crop quotas. State-fixed crop prices were often lower than prices on the open market, giving peasants little incentive to produce surpluses to sell.

Cadres, who were in charge of communes, often exhausted their soil and supplies.
". . . decisions have tended to be taken without consideration of their economic efficiency-for example, the generalized pursuit of higher grain-yields has in many cases been achieved only at the cost of reduced profitability and income. . . . Many low-level rural cadres are said to have preferred to implement directives blindly. Others are criticized for "feudal" or corrupt leadership."
​    - The Role of the People's Commune in Rural Development in China, Greg O'Leary & Andrew Watson
Poor infrastructure and transportation in rural areas led to an urban bias in regards to food distribution, causing peasants to starve. 

Under the pressure of utopian expectations, cadres and government officials concealed their declining production, boasting of higher output and hiding the famine conditions that ravaged their communes. ​
Starving peasants after "Chinese Civil War"

Treatment of Laborers

Public beating on commune
In addition to poor results, communal farms were often hostile and dangerous environments due to the treatment of laborers. 
Between 2 and 3 million of these victims were tortured to death or summarily executed, often for the slightest infraction. . . . One report dated Nov. 30, 1960, and circulated to the top leadership – most likely including Mao –tells how a man named Wang Ziyou had one of his ears chopped off, his legs tied up with iron wire and a 10-kilogram stone dropped on his back before he was branded with a sizzling tool. His crime: digging up a potato.
- Mao's Great Leap to Famine, Frank Dikotter

Steel Production and Heavy Industry

In preparation for industrialization, Mao emphasized steel production using "backyard furnaces". 

. . . ninety million people smelted steel and iron in six hundred thousand backyard furnaces, melting down cooking pots, bicycle frames, and doorknobs. Through stupendous effort they doubled China's steel output. Most of it, however, was brittle and worthless.
- J. R. McNeill, The Great Acceleration
It [backyard steel campaign] was accelerated in the summer to the fulfillment of the unrealistic steel output target of 10.70 million tonnes set for that year. The mass mobilization involved left wide stretches of farmland either unsown or unattended. . . . this resulted in a marked decrease in the grain-sown area in 1958.
- Y. Y. Kueh, Agricultural Instability in China (1931-1991)

Famine

The "Great Famine" was a result of poor commune management, the emphasis on steel production, and poor weather. This famine killed an estimated 30 million people by starvation.

Documents report several thousand cases where people ate other people. Parents ate their own kids. Kids ate their own parents. And we couldn't have imagined there was still grain in the warehouses. At the worst time, the government was still exporting grain."
                                                  - Yang Jisheng, Tombstone – The Great Chinese Famine
