​The "household responsibility system" was initiated in 1979 and served as a transition away from communal farming.

Inspiration
The responsibility system was first inspired by farmers in Anhui who began farming in individual households. Initially, central authorities limited this agricultural style to poorer regions.
Flood damage in Anhui
". . . a small number of production teams in Anhui province, located in areas victimized by flood and drought, first secretly and later with the blessing of local authorities began to try out the system of contracting land, other resources, and output quotas to individual households. A year later, these teams brought in yields far larger than those of other teams in the same areas."
- Justin Yifu Lin, The Household Responsibility System: An Theoretical and Empirical Study
It is to Deng Xiaoping's credit that when he discovered this [household farming] . . . he recognized that this was an effective system to implement throughout China. 
- Dr. Lei Guang, Director of the 21st Century China Center and UCSD Professor of Political Science

Marketing of Surpluses

Deng-era market
The Chinese government gradually dropped specific crop requirements. By 1984, the peasants were free to grow the crops they chose with the exception of grain and cotton.
Deng enabled peasants who exceeded their crop quotas to sell surpluses, also raising state-fixed crop prices to make their goods marketable.

Landowning Autonomy

In the early 1980s, the Chinese government allowed began allowing peasants to rent and lease land. A government decree in 1984 allowed for 15 year land contracts. In the 1990s, most contracts were extended to 30 years. This created the notion of autonomy among peasants despite the fact that all land was officially state-owned.
". . . the government will establish markets where farmers can “subcontract, lease, exchange or swap” land-use rights or join cooperatives. . . . allowing leasing or transfer would enable the creation of larger, more efficient farms that could increase output."
- Jim Yardley, China Enacts Major Land-Use Reform (2008)

Managing Autonomy

On top of allowing peasants to decide what to grow, Chinese government also freed up agricultural labor, allowing household managers decide how many and which laborers they hired. This helped balance out the disproportionate amount of excess rural labor. Peasants could also distribute their own tools and resources.
". . . peasants were taking things into their own hands, dividing up the commune land, and basically making decisions about production and allocation of labor on their own. . . . The responsibility system returned agricultural decisions to peasant households rather than commune officials. Now, individual households could make decisions about what to grow, when to grow, and how many laborers they need."
 - Dr. Lei Guang
