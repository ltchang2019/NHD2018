Breaking the Ideological Barrier

By welcoming foreign advice and allowing looser adherence to socialist expectations, Deng allowed China to move towards a freer, capitalist economy.

Township and Village Enterprises (TVEs)

With the expanding abolition of communes and incoming foreign investment, townships and villages gained the freedom to decide what goods produce and how to produce them.
"In the rural reform, our greatest success—and it is one we by no means anticipated—has been the emergence of a large number of enterprises run by townships and villages. They were like a new force that just came into being spontaneously."
- Deng Xiaoping, Talk with Yugoslav Official (1987)
Huaxi, Jiangsu province (a former TVE)
Rather than suppress this transition away from state-owned enterprises, he allowed TVEs to continue growing, prioritizing economic growth over socialist ideals. 
"TVEs were completely flexible in adapting to market conditions. . . . TVEs could draw on young labor and avoid the higher pay and extensive welfare payments that went to an older workforce. . . . Deng continued to avoid public statements, a strategy that allowed private enterprises to grow in a way that would not alarm conservative officials."
​​- Ezra F. Vogel, Deng Xiaoping and the Transformation of China

Embracing Foreign Influence

Deng Xiaoping gives autograph to first students admitted to college through the college entrance exam

Exchange Students

Postdating Mao, who strictly closed off Western influence, Deng was faced with a general apprehension toward adopting foreign practices. Understanding what could be learned from more developed countries, Deng sought to accept influence from abroad.
"We are going to send thousands or tens of thousands of students to receive overseas education. . . . they should love their own motherland, and make it stronger and more developed. If they want to start a career, they should come back to China."
- Deng Xiaoping (Xinhua News Agency August 16, 2004)
In addition to students, Deng sent his own officials abroad to meet with economists, businessmen, and politicians to take in ideas and perspectives on how China could progress.
Zhao Ziyang (Premier of China under Deng)

Joining the World Bank

Robert McNamara
After Deng met with World Bank president Richard McNamara, China became a member of the organization, welcoming global economic and political advice and connections. Their guidance was crucial to China's growth. 
". . . the World Bank could bring global knowledge to China – how to appraise and implement priority projects; how to encourage innovation and introduce new technologies; how to develop institutions and policy instruments needed for good economic management. . ."
- Robert B. Zoellick, World Bank News (2010)
The bank report suggested that more attention should be paid to the use of prices to promote both more efficient investment decisions and make possible greater efficiency in the use of labor. . . . it also advocated that changes in prices and other reforms should not be made too quickly. . . The World Bank concluded that China could reach the goal [quadrupling economic output in two decades] by concentrating on industrial production or by promoting more balanced development of other sectors including services; China chose to concentrate on industry.
- Ezra F. Vogel, Deng Xiaoping and the Transformation of China

Growth vs. Ideology

With many fearing elimination of Mao's legacy, Deng drew a line between adopting beneficial economic policies and prompting the abandonment of socialist practices, reassuring the Chinese people that growth was not capitalism and that stagnation was not socialism. He argued for the creation of a unique Chinese socialism that integrated certain facets of capitalism.
"It doesn't matter whether the cat is black or white, so long as it catches mice."
- Deng Xiaoping, Speech at Communist Youth League conference (July 7, 1962)
"To uphold socialism we must eliminate poverty. Poverty is not socialism."
 - Deng Xiaoping, Talk with Japanese delegation (June 30, 1984)
​Caption:
"To integrate the universal truth of Marxism with the concrete reality of our country and to blaze a path of our own and build socialism with Chinese characteristics is the basic conclusion we draw from our long history."
 - Deng Xiaoping, speech at the 12th National Congress of the Communist Party (September 1982)
