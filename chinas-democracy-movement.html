The Democracy Movement

Context to the Democracy Movement

Following the death of Mao came a period of unrestrained criticism of the Chinese communist regime. Criticism was largely in response to the suppressive use of state power during the Cultural Revolution.
“. . . all across the land, there were arrests everywhere, suppressions everywhere, miscarriages of justice everywhere. . . . This was the rehearsal of social-fascism in our country. "
- Li Yi Zhe Manifesto, On Socialist Democracy and the Legal System (1974)
"On Socialist Democracy and the Legal System"
Activist organizations included The Beijing Spring, The Chinese Human Rights League, The Enlightenment Society, Masses' Reference News, and others.
Democracy Wall Posters, Beijing
Criticism was expressed through posters (dazibao), ​underground journalists, and physical protests.
First are those criticizing Mao and other leaders. . . Second are the personal complaints about arbitrary political persecution and miscarriages of justice. . . Third are calls for democratic reforms, a socialist legal system, and human rights."
​- Kjeld Erik Brodsgaard, The Democracy Movement in China
The Democracy Movement was launched by some youths who felt that the system in our socialist society remained far from perfect and that there were imperfections in our legal system. . . . they came up with many proposals for democracy and the legal system. This paper was born out of a desire to further develop and perpetuate this movement. . . . to meet the needs of current political struggles, and to turn the poster into a more effective mass medium. 
- 1978 Interview with Xu Wenli (April Fifth Forum editor)

Democracy Wall​

Located in Xidan, Beijing, Democracy Wall allowed thousands to protest social and political issues. Deng initially supported the wall when criticism was aimed at Mao and the Gang of Four.
"these authors [of greivances] nevertheless spoke for a larger group -the 17 million young people banished from the cities - and for aspirations felt by many others."
- Politics and Poems (NY Times), David Lattimore
Some even posted poems on the wall to express their feelings of pain and will power.
Beneath a sheet of frozen ice,
fish drift with the flow,
One cannot hear their painful sighs. . .
​- Shi Zhi, "A Story of Fish" 
. . . their nature so persistent,
​Ardently thirsting after freedom and the sun,
They leap from the water. . .
​​- Shi Zhi, "A Story of Fish"
"Starting to write as the Cultural Revolution was taking shape, Shi Zhi appears today as an early forerunner to the changes in Chinese poetry that began to emerge during that time of repression and that have now come to represent the Chinese present."
- Zhang Qinghua, Editor of Winter Sun (collection of Shi Zhi's poems)
