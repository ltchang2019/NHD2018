Economic Decline

Private to Public Property​

During the 1950s, Mao undertook the task of converting China's private economy into a public one, where all property belonged to the government.
Within a few short years, privately owned commercial and industrial firms . . . were de facto nationalized. . . . "national capitalism" was coming to an abrupt end in the cities, private ownership of land was being abolished in the countryside.
​– Maurice Meisner (An Inquiry Into the Fate of Chinese Socialism)
. . . China, under the leadership of Chairman Mao Zedong, maintained a centrally planned, or command, economy. A large share of the country's economic output was directed and controlled by the state, which set production goals, controlled prices, and allocated resources throughout most of the economy. . . . Private enterprises and foreign investment firms were generally barred. A central goal of the Chinese government was to make China's economy relatively self sufficient. Foreign trade was generally limited to only obtaining those goods that could not be made or obtained in China.
- Wayne M. Morrison, China's Economic Rise: History, Trends, Challenges, and Implications for the United States,
With minimal emphasis on profitability and competition, China's economic growth was stunted, causing widespread poverty and declining standards of living.

Stagnation

Exemplifying China’s economic stagnation was the fact that its growth in GDP per capita, which generally represents the overall financial status of the country's citizens, had fallen substantially lower than that of its Southeast Asian counterparts.
 Between 1950 and 1978, Japan’s GDP per capita grew from around $2,000 per capita to over $12,000 per capita. Taiwan too experienced economic growth, seeing its GDP per capita grow by around $2,750 between 1950 and 1973. In contrast, China’s growth nearly flatlined, only growing by $400 during the same duration of time.
Comparison of Japanese and Chinese GDP per Capita (1950-1978)
Government policies kept the Chinese economy relatively stagnant and inefficient, mainly because most aspects of the economy were managed and run by the central government (and thus there were few profit incentives for firms, workers, and farmers), competition was virtually non existent, foreign trade and investment flows were mainly limited to Soviet bloc countries, and price and production controls caused widespread distortion in the economy.
- Wayne M. Morrison, China's Economic Rise: History, Trends, Challenges, and Implications for the United States
Average Yearly Wages per Capita (Trading Economics)

Low Wages

The average wages per capita too displayed the shortcomings of Mao's command economy, as no significant growth in yearly wages was seen from the time of Mao's reforms to Deng Xiaoping's rise to power. 

Pervasive Inequality
Following the Soviet model of industrialization, Mao's First Five Year plan (1953) increased industrial production rate by 18%. As a result of industrialization, however, a growing welfare gap between industrial workers and peasants emerged.
. . . social status, mobility opportunities, ways of life, and even basic citizenship claims of China's rural versus urban residents diverged sharply under the socialist system that Mao and his colleagues created, producing a caste like division that did not exist before 1949.
- Rural and Urban Inequalities in Contemporary China, Martin King Whyte
